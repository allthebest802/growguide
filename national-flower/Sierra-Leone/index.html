National Flower of Sierra Leone

Longhorn Orchid — Ancistrochilus rothschildianus

The national flower of Sierra Leone is the Longhorn Orchid — Ancistrochilus rothschildianus — a rare and beautiful epiphytic orchid native to the tropical forests of West Africa. Sierra Leone has a rich botanical heritage: its forests contain thousands of plant species, including many rare orchids, palms and tropical trees found nowhere else. The Longhorn Orchid was named after the Rothschild family, prominent patrons of botanical exploration in the nineteenth century.

🇬🇧 UK Growing Tip A specialist heated greenhouse orchid. Minimum 15°C in winter, 18°C in summer. High humidity essential. Grow mounted on cork bark. Available from orchid specialist nurseries.

What does the Longhorn Orchid look like?

Ancistrochilus rothschildianus is an epiphytic orchid — it grows on the bark and branches of trees rather than in soil, collecting moisture and nutrients from the humid forest air. It produces pseudobulbs (swollen stems that store water and nutrients) from which emerge attractive pink and white flowers. The blooms have distinctive elongated petals and a lip (labellum) marked with deeper pink or purple. Flowers appear in autumn and winter in its native habitat. The plant is highly prized by orchid collectors worldwide for its unusual form and attractive colouring.

Sierra Leone's botanical heritage

Sierra Leone is one of West Africa's most botanically diverse countries. The Gola Rainforest — one of the largest remaining tracts of Upper Guinea rainforest — contains extraordinary plant diversity, including numerous orchid species, ancient ironwood trees, and plants used in traditional medicine for centuries. This forest is now a national park shared with Liberia. The Longhorn Orchid as national flower reflects Sierra Leone's identity as a country with extraordinary natural wealth, much of it still being documented by botanists.

Growing Longhorn Orchid in the UK

Ancistrochilus rothschildianus is a specialist orchid for experienced growers with heated greenhouse facilities. It requires year-round warmth (minimum 18°C in summer, 15°C in winter), high humidity, bright indirect light and careful watering. It is typically grown mounted on cork bark or in a basket with coarse bark compost, allowing excellent drainage around the roots. Water and mist frequently in summer; reduce watering in winter but never allow to desiccate completely. The plant is occasionally available from specialist orchid nurseries and orchid societies in the UK.
