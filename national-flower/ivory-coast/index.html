National Flower of Ivory Coast

Flame Lily — Gloriosa superba

The national flower of Ivory Coast (Côte d'Ivoire) is the Flame Lily — Gloriosa superba. One of the most spectacular flowers in the plant world, the Flame Lily produces extraordinary blooms with reflexed petals in fiery red and yellow, curled back to reveal prominent stamens. It is also the national flower of Zimbabwe, reflecting its wide distribution across sub-Saharan Africa.

🇬🇧 UK Growing Tip Plant tubers horizontally 10cm deep after last frosts. Give support to climb. Lift and store dry in winter. Wear gloves — all parts are toxic.

What does the Flame Lily look like?

Gloriosa superba is a climbing plant that scrambles through vegetation using tendril-like leaf tips. Its flowers are unlike almost anything else in the plant world — the six petals are strongly reflexed (curled backwards), displaying vivid red and yellow colouring and prominent, widely spreading stamens. The effect is of a flower turned inside out, almost flame-like in its upward sweep of colour. Flowers appear in succession throughout summer on established plants.

Why is the Flame Lily the national flower of Ivory Coast?

The Flame Lily was chosen as a symbol of Côte d'Ivoire's rich tropical flora and its connection to the wider African botanical world. Gloriosa superba grows wild in the tropical forests and savannahs of West Africa, where it has been used in traditional medicine for centuries. Its striking appearance makes it instantly recognisable as a symbol of natural beauty and strength — qualities that align with the national identity Ivory Coast wishes to project internationally.

Growing Flame Lily in the UK

Gloriosa superba can be grown successfully in UK gardens as a tender summer climber. Plant the elongated tubers in spring, after the last frost, in a warm sheltered spot with well-drained soil. The tubers are planted horizontally about 10cm deep. Provide a cane or trellis for support — the plant climbs actively and can reach 1.5–2 metres. Lift and store the tubers in dry compost over winter, as they will not survive UK frosts in the ground. Important: all parts of Gloriosa superba are highly toxic if ingested, particularly the tubers — handle with gloves and keep away from children and pets.

Flor nacional de Costa de Marfil

La flor nacional de Costa de Marfil (Côte d'Ivoire) es el Lirio Llama (Gloriosa superba), una planta trepadora de origen tropical africano con flores extraordinarias de pétalos curvados en rojo y amarillo. También es la flor nacional de Zimbabue. Todas las partes de la planta son tóxicas si se ingieren.
