National Flower of Ghana

Impala Lily — Adenium obesum

The national flower of Ghana is the Impala Lily — Adenium obesum, also widely known as the Desert Rose despite being unrelated to true roses. A dramatic succulent plant native to the dry savannah regions of sub-Saharan Africa, the Impala Lily produces striking funnel-shaped flowers in shades of pink, red and white, emerging from a swollen, sculptural stem that stores water through the dry season.

🇬🇧 UK Growing Tip South-facing windowsill, cactus compost, water freely in summer. Almost no water October–March. Keep above 10°C. Feed with tomato fertiliser in summer.

What does the Impala Lily look like?

Adenium obesum develops a dramatically swollen, grey-green caudex (stem base) that can reach enormous proportions on mature specimens, giving the plant a bonsai-like sculptural quality even when small. From this swollen base, slender branches bear glossy green leaves and clusters of funnel-shaped flowers in pink, red, white or bicolour combinations. The flowers have five rounded petals and a slightly darker throat. In its native habitat, flowering occurs at the end of the dry season when leaves are absent, making the blooms even more spectacular against bare branches.

Why is the Impala Lily Ghana's national flower?

The Impala Lily is widely cultivated across Ghana as an ornamental plant in gardens, courtyards and pots, valued for its beauty, drought-tolerance and architectural presence. It has cultural significance across West Africa and is used in traditional medicine. Ghana chose it as a national symbol reflecting both the country's natural landscape — the savannah and semi-arid northern regions where Adenium grows wild — and the resilience and beauty that characterise Ghanaian identity.

Growing Impala Lily in the UK

In the UK, the Impala Lily is grown as an exotic houseplant or in a warm greenhouse. It needs maximum sun — a south-facing windowsill is ideal. Use very free-draining cactus compost or mix standard compost with grit or perlite at a 50:50 ratio. Water freely in summer when in active growth, but reduce to very occasional watering from October to March — overwatering in winter is the most common cause of death. Keep frost-free at all times; ideal winter minimum is 10°C. Feed with a low-nitrogen fertiliser (tomato feed works well) during the growing season to encourage flowering.

Flor nacional de Ghana

La flor nacional de Ghana es el Lirio del Impala (Adenium obesum), también conocido como Rosa del Desierto. Es una suculenta de tronco engrosado con flores en forma de embudo de color rosa, rojo y blanco. Se cultiva ampliamente en Ghana como planta ornamental y tiene importancia en la medicina tradicional africana. En el Reino Unido puede cultivarse como planta de interior en un lugar muy soleado.
